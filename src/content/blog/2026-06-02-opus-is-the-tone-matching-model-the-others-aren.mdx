There's a pattern showing up in enterprise AI that hasn't filtered down to marketing teams yet. Legal platforms doing serious drafting work, the kind where a partner will redline you for using "utilize" instead of "use," are standardizing on Claude Opus specifically for tone work. Not for reasoning. Not for speed. For tone.

That's a signal worth taking seriously, because if Opus is good enough to mimic the prose style of a specific litigator across a 40-page brief, it's almost certainly the right tool for matching your brand voice on a landing page. And brand voice is where most AI-generated marketing copy still falls apart.

## Why tone is harder than it looks

Most marketers I talk to have given up on AI for voice-sensitive work. They use it for outlines, research, first-pass structure, then rewrite everything by hand because the output sounds like a LinkedIn post written by a committee.

The problem isn't the model's vocabulary. It's that tone lives in a thousand small decisions: sentence length variance, where you place the verb, whether you use contractions, how often you break a rule on purpose. GPT-class models tend to regress to a median professional voice no matter how hard you prompt them. Opus seems to hold onto the specific cadence of whatever you feed it.

I don't have a clean technical explanation for why. My working theory is that Anthropic's training emphasis on long-context fidelity translates into better stylistic mimicry, because style is essentially long-range pattern matching across a document.

## What legal teams figured out that marketers haven't

The legal use case is instructive. A lawyer doesn't want generic legalese. They want their partner's specific style, or the firm's house style, or the style of the contract being amended. The bar is brutal: if the AI draft reads even slightly differently from the surrounding paragraphs, a senior associate spots it immediately.

So legal AI teams built their stack around the model that handles this best. They didn't pick on price. They didn't pick on benchmark scores. They picked on one specific capability: holding a voice across long, structured output.

Marketers have the same problem and a worse track record solving it. The "brand voice document" most teams hand to writers is usually three adjectives and a list of words to avoid. That's not enough scaffolding for any model, including Opus.

## The setup that actually works

Here's what I've been testing for client work over the last few weeks.

Instead of a brand voice prompt, give Opus three to five full pieces of real reference copy. Not snippets. Full pages. Email sequences, sales pages, founder posts, whatever the actual artifact is. Then ask it to write the new piece in the style of those references, with explicit instructions to match sentence length distribution and rhythm, not just vocabulary.

The output gets noticeably closer to usable on the first pass. Not perfect. But the gap between draft and final shrinks from "rewrite this entirely" to "tighten paragraphs three and seven."

Two specifics that matter:

The reference docs should be your best work, not your average work. Opus mimics what you give it. Feed it mediocre copy and you get mediocre copy back, just in your voice.

Long context is doing the heavy lifting here. If you truncate references to save tokens, you lose the rhythm signal. Pay for the full context. The cost difference per draft is pennies compared to an hour of rewriting.

## Where this breaks

Opus is not magic. A few honest failure modes I've hit:

It still over-explains. If your brand voice is terse, you'll need to prompt explicitly against elaboration, and even then it adds connective tissue you didn't ask for.

It struggles with intentional rule-breaking. If your voice uses fragments aggressively or violates grammar on purpose, Opus tends to "correct" toward standard prose. You can fight this with examples, but it's a known weak spot.

And it's expensive enough that you don't want to run it on every blog post. The right deployment is high-stakes copy: sales pages, founder emails, anything signed by a real person. Use cheaper models for utility work.

The legal industry pays Opus rates because the cost of bad tone is a partner rewriting your draft. The marketing equivalent is a CEO killing your campaign because "this doesn't sound like us." Same problem, same solution, much smaller budget allocated to fixing it.

If you're running content for a brand with a distinctive founder voice, this is where I'd spend my next test budget. Pick the three pieces of copy that most define how your brand sounds. Build a single Opus drafting workflow around them, with the full text in context every time. Track how much editing time drops on the next ten pieces. My guess is you'll move from rewriting 70% of the draft to rewriting 20%, which is the difference between AI being a toy and being a tool. The catch most people will miss: this only works if someone on your team can actually articulate, in writing, what makes your voice yours. The model can mimic, but it can't decide.
