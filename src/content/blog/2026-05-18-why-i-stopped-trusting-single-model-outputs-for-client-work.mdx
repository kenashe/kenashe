For a while I treated whichever model I was paying for that month as the answer. Claude was my main driver. GPT was the fallback. I'd write a prompt, get an output, edit it, ship it. Done.

That stopped working about three months ago when a client caught a confidently wrong claim in a draft I'd pushed through Claude 3.5. Nothing terrible. A misattributed quote. But it shook me enough to change how I work.

## The cross-check workflow

Now, for anything client-facing or anything where being wrong has a cost, I run the same prompt through at least two models. Usually Claude and GPT-5. Sometimes Gemini if the task involves long-context document reading where Google's 2M window actually matters.

Then I compare. Not the prose. The claims.

Where do they agree? Where do they disagree? Where does one model hedge and the other state something as fact? Those gaps are where I do my own verification.

This sounds like more work. It is. But it's caught things I would have shipped: invented statistics, wrong product features, attributed quotes that don't exist, and at least two cases of plausible-sounding industry jargon that turned out to be hallucinated.

## What the models are actually good at

After running this for a few months, patterns emerge.

Claude tends to be more cautious about specific factual claims and better at structured reasoning. It also writes prose I prefer, less filler, fewer hedges. When it does hallucinate, it's usually in confident-sounding analogies or framings that don't quite hold up.

GPT-5 is faster and more willing to commit to specifics. Sometimes that's great. Sometimes it's a problem because the specifics are wrong. It's also noticeably better at tool use and structured outputs when I'm building automations in n8n.

Gemini is the one I trust least for prose but most for "read this 80-page PDF and tell me what's in section 4." The context window genuinely changes what's possible there.

The point isn't ranking them. The point is that the disagreements between them are signal. If two models give me different numbers for the same claim, at least one is wrong, and possibly both.

## Where this falls apart

The workflow doesn't scale to every task. If I'm using AI to draft a quick internal Slack message or rewrite a sentence, I'm not running it through three models. The overhead would kill the productivity gain that's the whole point.

It also doesn't help when all the models are wrong in the same way. This happens more than I'd like. They've all been trained on overlapping data, and shared misconceptions show up as shared confidence. Anything about a niche tool, a recent product launch, or a specific person's stated position is suspect even when models agree.

The honest fix there is the boring one: check primary sources. Pull up the company's docs. Read the actual blog post. Watch the actual demo. AI is not going to replace that step for high-stakes claims, and I've stopped pretending it will.

## What changed in how I price work

A side effect I didn't expect: this workflow changed how I scope projects.

When I quote a content engagement now, I build in time for cross-model verification on anything that names a person, cites a number, or makes a technical claim. It's maybe 15% more time than my old single-model workflow. Clients have not pushed back on this once when I explain why.

The framing matters. I don't sell it as "AI quality control." I sell it as "fact-checking, because LLMs hallucinate." That's a sentence clients understand. The model orchestration is just how I do the work.

## The meta-lesson

The thing I keep coming back to: treating one model as the source of truth was a habit I picked up from search. Google gave me one answer, I clicked the top link, I moved on. LLMs feel like that, one box, one answer, but they're not that. They're more like a confident intern who read a lot and remembers most of it.

You wouldn't ship the intern's first draft without reading it. You probably wouldn't trust the intern's memory of a quote without checking. Running cross-checks is just doing that, but with the second intern being another LLM.

If you're shipping AI-assisted work to paying clients and you're using one model, this is the cheapest upgrade you can make. Open a second tab. Paste the same prompt. Read both outputs side by side and circle the claims that disagree. The first time you catch a hallucination this way, you'll never go back. The catch most people miss: the value isn't in picking the better output, it's in the disagreement itself, because that's where your verification budget should go.
