Most people I talk to are using Claude the same way they used ChatGPT in 2023. Ask a question. Get a paragraph. Copy it somewhere. Done.

That's fine for brainstorming. It's a waste of what the tool can actually do now. The interesting work, the work that compounds, happens when Claude stops talking and starts producing files on your computer.

## The shift from answers to artifacts

There's a quiet line that gets crossed when you let Claude execute code and touch your local file system. Suddenly the output isn't text in a chat window. It's a populated `.xlsx` with formulas. It's a `.pptx` with your sections already laid out. It's a Word doc with tracked changes against a brief you uploaded.

For a marketer, that's the difference between "Claude helped me think about a campaign recap" and "Claude shipped the recap deck I'll send to the client at 4pm."

The mental model I use: chat is for figuring out what you want. Code execution and computer use are for producing the thing.

## What this looks like for messy marketing inputs

The job I keep coming back to is synthesis. Marketing teams sit on piles of unstructured stuff: call notes, GA exports, a competitor teardown someone wrote in Notion, three different briefs from three different stakeholders, last quarter's roadmap, a Loom transcript, a customer interview.

The old workflow: someone (probably me) reads it all, makes a doc, makes a deck, makes a sheet.

The new workflow I've been testing:

1. Drop 15 to 30 source documents into a Claude project.
2. Describe the output I want. Not "summarize this" but "build me an Excel sheet with tabs for channel performance, audience themes, and Q3 recommendations, with the recommendations tab pulling supporting evidence from the source docs into a notes column."
3. Let Claude generate the actual file. Download. Open in Excel. Adjust.

The file I get back is not perfect. But it's 80% of the structural work done, and the 20% I'm fixing is the part where I add judgment, which is the part I should be doing anyway.

## Where PowerPoint changes the math

Decks are the high-leverage one. A monthly client report deck is maybe four hours of work for most accounts: pulling numbers, writing bullets, formatting slides, picking screenshots, writing the narrative.

If I give Claude the raw exports plus last month's deck as a template, I can get a draft `.pptx` with the structure, numbers, and a first pass at commentary. The narrative still needs my pass. The slide design still needs cleanup. But I'm starting from a 40% draft instead of a blank file.

The catch: Claude is not a designer. The decks it produces look functional, not beautiful. If your agency sells on visual polish, you still need a designer in the loop. If your client just wants the numbers and the story, you're fine.

## Why Excel is the underrated piece

Everyone gets excited about decks. Excel is the one I think is actually underpriced.

Claude is good at taking a column of qualitative inputs (open-ended survey responses, support tickets, sales call notes) and producing a structured sheet: themes, frequencies, example quotes, suggested follow-ups. That's a task that used to mean buying a tool or paying an analyst.

Now it's a file on your desktop in 90 seconds. The formulas work. The pivot tables work. You can hand the sheet to a teammate who has never used Claude and they can just open it.

That last part matters more than people admit. Outputs that live inside the chat window die there. Outputs that exist as files travel through your org.

## The honest limits

A few things I keep running into:

Token limits still apply. If you dump 200 PDFs in, you'll hit context ceilings and Claude will start dropping things. I've had better luck batching: 20 docs, get a synthesis file, then 20 more, then merge.

Citations are inconsistent. When Claude pulls a quote or stat from one of your source docs into a cell, it doesn't always tag which doc it came from. For client work where the source matters, prompt explicitly: "include a source column with the filename."

It will confidently produce wrong numbers. Always spot-check at least three values in any sheet it generates against the source. I've caught Claude inventing totals that looked plausible but didn't add up.

If I were a digital marketing operator looking at this today, here's the bet I'd make: spend a week rebuilding one recurring deliverable (a monthly recap, a competitive scan, a campaign brief) as a Claude workflow that outputs a file, not a chat response. Pick the deliverable you hate doing most. The goal isn't to remove yourself from the work, it's to move your hours from formatting to judgment. The agencies that figure out this layer in 2026 are going to look at the agencies still copy-pasting from chat windows the way we look at agencies that don't use Looker Studio.
