When a new Claude or GPT version ships, engineering teams at AI-native companies do something specific within hours: they kick off automated evals against a stored set of tasks and watch the pass rate change. They are not asking the model to write a haiku and squinting at the output. They have a graded test suite, and the suite tells them whether to switch.

Marketing teams do the opposite. A new model drops, someone on Twitter says it's amazing, the team paste in one prompt, eyeball the result, and either shrug or migrate the whole workflow over. That's a vibe check. It's how you end up six months in on a model that's quietly worse at your actual job than the one you replaced.

## What an eval actually is, in marketing terms

An eval is just three things: an input, an expected quality signal, and a way to grade. Engineers run thousands of these. You only need 30 to 50 to get useful signal on marketing work.

For ad copy, an input might be a product brief plus an audience definition. The expected signal is something like: does the output mention the offer, stay under the character limit, avoid banned compliance phrases, and match brand voice on a 1 to 5 scale. For SEO briefs, the input is a target keyword and SERP context. The signal is whether the brief covers the entities competitors cover, suggests a defensible angle, and structures headers in a way an editor wouldn't rewrite.

Grading can be a second LLM acting as judge, a rubric you check by hand, or a regex for the boring stuff (length, banned words, required CTA). The judge model approach scales. You write the rubric once, then any new model gets scored against the same bar.

## Build the golden dataset from your own wins

This is the part nobody does and it's the entire point. Go back through the last 12 months of marketing output. Pull the 30 pieces that actually performed: the ad that hit a 4x ROAS, the SEO brief that produced a page ranking top three, the email that printed a 38% open rate, the landing page copy the client signed off on with zero edits.

For each one, save the original input you would have given an LLM: the brief, the audience, the constraints, the source research. Now you have a golden dataset. The input is what you'd ask the model. The known-good output is what worked in the real world.

When a new model arrives, you run those 30 inputs through it and compare. Is the new model's output closer to the proven winner, or further away? Did it invent claims? Did it strip the specificity that made the original convert?

This is the move. You are not asking "is this model good." You are asking "is this model good at the work I actually ship."

## The cadence that works

Run the suite the day a model becomes available on your provider. Log scores in a spreadsheet or a tiny Airtable. Track four columns: model version, average rubric score, regression count (places it got worse than the prior model), and surprise wins (places it got noticeably better).

What you'll find: most upgrades are sideways. Some are real for one task and worse for another. Claude Sonnet might pull ahead on long-form briefs while GPT holds the edge on snappy hooks. You stop having one default model. You start routing tasks.

The teams shipping AI products already think this way. Their dashboards show task success rates jumping 20% on a model swap, or quietly dropping on a regression. You can run the marketing version of that dashboard in a Google Sheet.

## Where this approach breaks

Two honest catches.

First, the golden dataset goes stale. Audience preferences shift. A creative pattern that crushed in Q1 2025 looks tired by Q2 2026. You need to refresh the dataset every six months, or you'll be optimizing models against yesterday's winners.

Second, evals reward what you can measure. Brand voice on a 1 to 5 scale captures a lot, but not everything. The model that scores best on your rubric might still produce work that feels subtly off in ways you can't articulate. Keep at least one senior human reviewer in the loop on the top 10% of outputs. Evals filter; humans still judge.

If I were starting today, I'd spend one afternoon assembling 20 inputs and known-good outputs from past wins, write a five-criterion rubric in plain English, and use Claude as a judge to score whatever model I'm currently running. That gives you a baseline. The day the next model ships, you have a real answer in 30 minutes about whether to switch, instead of an opinion based on three prompts and a feeling. The marketers who build this muscle now will spend the next two years routing work to the right model per task while everyone else is still asking which one is "the best."
