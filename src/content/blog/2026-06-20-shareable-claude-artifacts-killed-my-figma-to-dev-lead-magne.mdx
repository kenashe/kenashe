For the last two years, every interactive lead magnet I scoped followed the same path: idea, mockup, dev quote, four week wait, revisions, launch. Half the time the client lost interest before launch. The other half, the math in the calculator was wrong because the spec doc was ambiguous and nobody caught it.

That pipeline is now optional. Claude Artifacts shipped public share links, which means a working ROI calculator, quiz, or pricing tool can live at a URL within an hour of the kickoff call. Not a screenshot. Not a Loom. A working thing the client can click.

## What actually changed

Artifacts have existed for a while. You could build a calculator in Claude, see it render in the side panel, even export the code. The friction was distribution. To get it in front of a stakeholder, you either screen-shared, sent the code to a dev, or pasted it into a CodeSandbox. None of those are how marketers move.

Now each artifact gets a shareable link. The recipient does not need a Claude account to view it. They click, they use it, they tell you what's wrong. That last part is the unlock. Iteration loops on interactive assets used to take days. Now they take the length of a Slack reply.

## The lead magnet use case

Interactive content converts better than static content. That's been true since BuzzFeed quizzes. The problem has always been cost: a custom ROI calculator from a dev shop runs $4k to $15k, and a personality quiz with branching logic on Typeform plus a designer plus a writer is not cheap either.

Here is what I've been building in an afternoon each:

A B2B SaaS ROI calculator that takes seat count, current tool cost, and hours saved per week, then outputs annualized savings with a chart. Three input fields, one output, one CTA button.

A "what's your marketing maturity stage" quiz with eight questions and four possible outcomes, each with a custom recommendation paragraph.

A pricing configurator for a client who sells services in tiered packages. Pick three options, get a quote range, book a call.

Every one of these used to be a four-figure invoice and a month of calendar time. Now they're a prompt, a few revisions, and a link.

## Where it works and where it breaks

Works: prototypes for client pitches, internal stakeholder buy-in, A/B testing concepts before committing to a real build, lead magnets for short campaigns where the asset doesn't need to live forever.

Breaks: anything that needs to write to a database, integrate with a CRM, or capture leads in a way that ties to your existing marketing stack. Artifacts run in a sandboxed iframe. They can't post to your HubSpot endpoint. They can't fire a Zapier webhook. They can collect email in a form field, but that data goes nowhere unless you wire it up.

The workaround I've been using: the artifact captures the result, then directs the user to a Tally or Typeform with the calculated value prefilled via URL parameters. The Artifact does the interactive math. The form does the capture. Ugly but it works.

The other catch: branding. The shared artifact lives on Anthropic's domain. For client work, this matters. I treat the link as a prototype URL, not a production URL. Once the client approves the logic and design, I either rebuild it as a real component on their site or hand the code to a dev who wraps it properly. The artifact is the spec, not the deliverable.

## What this does to the agency model

If you're a marketing consultant or small agency, this collapses a service line that used to be profitable. Interactive content as a standalone offering at $5k a pop is harder to defend when the client knows their ops person could build a version of it themselves.

Where it goes instead: the strategic layer. Which calculator drives the most demos? What questions in a quiz produce the best segmentation for downstream nurture? What's the right offer to put at the end? The build is now cheap. The thinking about what to build is the same as it ever was, which means it's worth more relative to the total project cost.

If you're shipping client work this quarter, I'd pick one interactive concept you've been wanting to test for a client and just build it tonight. Don't pitch it first. Send the working link cold. The conversation that comes back will tell you more about whether interactive content fits their funnel than any deck would. The catch most people will miss: the value isn't the calculator itself, it's that you've moved from selling a deliverable to selling a working prototype before any money changes hands. That changes who closes and how fast.
