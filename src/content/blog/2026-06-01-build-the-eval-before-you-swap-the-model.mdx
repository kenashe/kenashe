When a new Claude or GPT version ships, the teams building real products don't sit there asking the model trivia questions. They kick off automated eval runs and watch a dashboard. Agent success rate goes from 62% to 81%. They know within an hour whether to migrate.

Most marketing operators I talk to do the opposite. New model drops, they paste in their favorite prompt, eyeball the output, say "feels smarter," and move on. That's vibe-checking. It's how you end up six months deep in a workflow built on a model that's already been lapped twice.

The leap from amateur to practitioner is building an eval suite for your own work. Here's how I'm thinking about it.

## What an eval actually is

An eval is a fixed test you can re-run against any model and score the same way every time. Three parts: an input, an expected shape of output, and a scoring method.

For a marketer, the input might be a client brief plus a target keyword. The expected output is an SEO brief with specific sections: search intent, competitor angle gaps, suggested H2s, internal link targets, word count range. The scoring method is what most people skip. You need a way to say "this output passed" or "this output failed" without re-reading it from scratch each time.

Scoring can be a rubric scored by another model (LLM-as-judge), a regex check for required sections, a comparison against a known-good answer, or a human pass/fail. Mix them. The point is it's repeatable.

## A starter suite for a marketing stack

If I'm running a content and SEO operation, my baseline eval set covers the workflows I actually depend on. Something like:

1. SEO brief generation. 20 real keywords from past clients, with a known-good brief for each. Score: does the new brief hit the same intent, cover the same competitor gaps, suggest a comparable structure.
2. Content synthesis from research dumps. 10 cases where I have a folder of 5-15 sources and a target angle. Score: factual accuracy (LLM judge against sources), angle adherence, no hallucinated stats.
3. Ad copy variants. 15 product briefs with known winning headlines from past tests. Score: does the model produce variants in the same quality band, plus a diversity check so it isn't just rewriting the same line.
4. Client email triage and drafting. 30 anonymized inbound emails. Score: correct intent classification, tone match, no fabricated commitments.
5. Long-context analysis. A few cases of "here are 50 pages of brand guidelines plus a campaign brief, produce a launch plan." Score: rubric across 8 dimensions.

That's it. Maybe 80 test cases total. You build it once over a weekend and it pays off for years.

## The dashboard is the unlock

The reason frontier teams move fast isn't that they're smarter. It's that they can see. A green-to-red dashboard showing 62% to 81% on agent success rate is the difference between "I think this is better" and "I know this is better, by exactly this much, on these specific tasks."

For a marketer, that dashboard answers the question every operator avoids: is my workflow actually getting better, or am I just buying new subscriptions? When Claude 4.5 or GPT-6 drops, you re-run the suite, you see brief generation jumped from 71% to 89%, you migrate that workflow. Meanwhile ad copy stayed flat at 78%, so you keep the old model there. Different jobs, different winners.

## Failing tests are the most valuable ones

Here's the part that took me a while to get. The tests that consistently fail are the ones you want most. They tell you exactly what the next model needs to be good at for your business to level up.

If my "synthesize 15 sources into a non-obvious angle" eval has been stuck at 40% for three model generations, that's a flag. Either the task is genuinely hard and I should prepare for a step change when it finally cracks, or my prompt is doing too much work and I need to break the workflow into smaller agentic steps. Either way, the failing eval told me where to look. A passing eval just tells you the model can do something. A failing one tells you where the frontier is, for you specifically.

## Where to start this week

Pick one workflow. The one you run most often or the one that costs you the most hours. Build 10 test cases with known-good outputs. Write a one-page rubric. Run it against your current model. Save the score. Next time a new model drops, run it again.

A marketer building eval discipline now is doing the same thing dev teams did with CI/CD ten years ago: investing in feedback loops while the rest of the field still ships on feel. The catch most people will miss is that the eval suite itself becomes the moat. Your prompts can be copied. Your tools can be subscribed to. But a curated set of 80 test cases that reflect your specific clients, your specific quality bar, and your specific failure modes is the thing nobody else has. That's what lets you swap models in a single afternoon while your competitors spend a quarter "evaluating options."
