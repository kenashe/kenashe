There's a quiet shift happening inside go-to-market teams at AI companies. AEs are stopping being AEs. They're becoming builders who happen to sell. The wedge that makes this possible is a specific pattern: system prompt, context retrieval, API call. Three pieces. That's the whole stack.

I want to break down how that pattern works for email drafting specifically, because email is where most account teams are bleeding hours, and because the architecture is small enough to actually ship.

## What the three-piece stack actually does

Most people using AI for email are doing one thing: pasting a thread into ChatGPT and asking for a reply. That works for generic responses. It falls apart the moment a customer asks something technical about your product, your pricing, your roadmap, or the specific integration they're stuck on.

The three-piece stack solves this by separating three concerns that prompt-pasting smashes together:

1. **Who you are and how you write.** That's the system prompt. Role, tone, the customer segments you handle, what you do and don't commit to in writing.
2. **What you know.** That's context retrieval. Your product docs, your internal Slack answers turned into a doc, your pricing page, your security FAQ.
3. **What just happened.** That's the email thread itself, passed in at runtime.

When you keep these separate, you can update one without breaking the others. Change your product positioning? Edit the system prompt. Ship a new feature? Add it to the context doc. Get a new email? The thread flows in. Nothing else changes.

## The system prompt is a job description, not a vibe

The mistake I see in early attempts is treating the system prompt like a personality. "You are a friendly, helpful assistant." Useless.

A system prompt for an email drafter should read like an internal job description. Who is the person sending the email. What's their role. What products are they responsible for. What are the three or four scenarios they handle most often (renewal, technical evaluation, expansion, escalation). What they should never do in writing (quote prices not in the deal desk doc, commit to roadmap dates, agree to custom legal terms).

If you write that prompt well, you've essentially encoded a junior AE's onboarding doc into a function call. That's the leverage.

## Context retrieval is where the real work lives

Here's the part most teams skip: the quality of your AI email drafter is almost entirely a function of the quality of the docs it pulls from.

Two patterns work well right now. One is pointing the API at a curated Google Doc that gets updated weekly. Boring, manual, effective. The other is letting the model fetch live URLs from your docs site at runtime. Slower per request, but always current.

The Claude API supports both. You can pass document content directly in the request, or use the URL fetch capability to grab pages on demand. For an internal tool, I'd start with a single Google Doc that contains your top 20 customer answers, your pricing tiers, your integration list, and your security posture summary. Ship that. Iterate.

The trap is trying to wire up a vector database and embeddings on day one. You don't need it. A 30-page Google Doc fits comfortably in a Claude context window. Use the simple thing until it breaks.

## Why this pattern beats a chatbot interface

A chat interface forces the user to do the retrieval work. They have to remember what to ask, what to paste, what context the model needs. A drafter inverts that. The user clicks "generate draft" on an email they already have open, and the system figures out what context to pull based on the thread content.

That inversion is the unlock. You're not asking the AE to be a prompt engineer 600 times a day. You're asking them once to set up the system, then letting it run.

This is also why I think the next wave of useful AI tools inside companies won't look like ChatGPT clones. They'll look like buttons inside the apps people already use. Generate draft. Summarize call. Draft renewal proposal. Each one with its own three-piece stack hiding underneath.

## What this means if you're in RevOps or marketing ops

If you've been waiting for permission to start building internal AI tools, the email drafter is the right first project. It's narrow, the ROI is measurable in hours saved per rep per day, and the architecture transfers to half a dozen other workflows once you've shipped it.

A practitioner's take: build the v1 yourself this week. One AE, one system prompt, one Google Doc of context, one API key. Don't pitch it to the team yet. Use it for five days on your own inbox. Measure how often you ship the draft unedited versus rewriting it. That edit rate is your real KPI, not "hours saved." Below 30% edits, you have something worth rolling out. Above 50%, your context doc is too thin and you need to go fix that before anyone else touches the tool. The companies that win the next 18 months aren't the ones with the best models. They're the ones whose internal docs are clean enough to be retrieved.
