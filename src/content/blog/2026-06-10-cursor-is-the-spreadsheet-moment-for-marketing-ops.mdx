For most of my career, building anything custom meant filing a request with engineering and waiting. A UTM audit script. A simple dashboard that pulled from three APIs. A scraper to monitor competitor pricing. Every one of these requests went into a backlog and most never came out.

That equation broke this year. I've been building small internal tools in Cursor for the last few months, and I'm not a developer. The work I'm shipping now would have been a Jira ticket six months ago.

## What changed in the tooling

Cursor is an IDE, which is the kind of word that makes marketers close the tab. Skip the label. What it actually does: you type what you want in plain English, and an agent writes, runs, and debugs the code in the same window. When something breaks, you paste the error back in and it fixes itself. The loop is fast enough that you can build a working script in an hour without knowing what a virtual environment is.

The reason this matters for marketers specifically is that most of our automation problems are small. We don't need to build Salesforce. We need a script that pulls last week's GSC data, joins it with GA4 sessions, and flags pages that lost more than 20% impressions. That's a 60-line Python file. A year ago it was inaccessible to me. Now it takes a coffee.

## The tools I've actually shipped

Three things I've built that earn their keep:

A keyword cannibalization checker that pulls our top 500 ranking URLs from Ahrefs, groups them by query, and flags overlap. Used to pay a freelancer $400 a month for a worse version.

A Slack bot that posts a daily summary of paid campaign anomalies. It hits the Google Ads API, compares spend and CPA against a 14-day baseline, and pings the channel if anything moves more than two standard deviations. Took me a Saturday.

A bulk meta description rewriter that takes a CSV of URLs, scrapes the page, sends content to Claude with our brand voice prompt, and outputs a new CSV. We process about 2,000 URLs a week through it now.

None of these are impressive as software. They're impressive as leverage. Each one replaced either a recurring vendor cost or a weekly hour of someone's time on my team.

## Where non-technical operators hit the ceiling

I want to be honest about where this falls apart, because the breathless "anyone can code now" posts skip this part.

Authentication is where most marketers will stall. Getting OAuth working with the Google Ads API the first time was painful. Cursor will write the code, but you still have to set up a Google Cloud project, enable the right APIs, generate credentials, and store them safely. The agent can guide you, but if you've never seen a JSON key file before, expect to lose an afternoon.

Anything that needs to run on a schedule is the second wall. Building a script is easy. Getting it to run every morning at 7am without your laptop being open requires a server, a cron job, or something like Replit or Railway. This isn't hard, but it's a separate skill stack.

And the third wall is the one nobody talks about: knowing what to build. The agent will build whatever you ask for. It won't tell you that the thing you're asking for is dumb, or that there's a SaaS tool that already does it for $30 a month. Taste and judgment are still on you.

## The new economics of an ops team

The way I now think about hiring on a marketing team has shifted. A year ago, "can you code" was a binary. Either you had a developer on the team or you didn't, and if you didn't, certain projects were off the table.

That binary is gone. The new question is whether someone on the team has the patience to iterate with an agent. That's a different skill, closer to project management than programming. The people on my team who are best at it are not the ones who took a Python course. They're the ones who write good briefs.

The implication for anyone running a marketing org: the ratio of tools you build versus tools you buy is about to flip. Most marketing SaaS exists because building was too expensive. That premise is gone for anything under maybe 500 lines of code. I'd start by auditing your current stack and asking which tools you're paying for that do one specific thing your team could now build in a weekend. Don't try to replace your CRM. Do replace the $200/month tool that just sends you a weekly report. That's where the first wins are, and you'll learn enough building them to spot the next ten.
