Ask anyone on a sales team what "last quarter" means and you'll get the fiscal answer. Ask product or engineering and you'll get the calendar answer. Same words, same company, different numbers. Now imagine you wired an LLM into your warehouse and let your CMO ask, "How did paid social do last quarter?" Which definition wins?

This is the boring, unglamorous reason most internal "chat with your data" projects fail. The model is brilliant at SQL. It is clueless about you.

## The metric ambiguity problem is bigger than dates

"Last quarter" is the easy example. The real mess in marketing data is worse:

Revenue means booked revenue to finance, recognized revenue to accounting, and pipeline-influenced revenue to demand gen. A "customer" might be an account in Salesforce, a billing entity in Stripe, and a user in Mixpanel. "Conversion" is a form fill, a trial start, or a closed deal depending on who's asking. "CAC" includes or excludes contractor spend depending on the spreadsheet.

A general-purpose LLM cannot resolve this. It will pick a plausible-sounding definition and hand you a number with full confidence. You will paste that number into a board deck. That is the failure mode.

## What a semantic layer actually does

The fix isn't a better prompt. It's a translation layer that sits between the question and the warehouse and encodes three things: which tables to use, how they join, and what the business actually calls these things.

If you have ever opened a real company warehouse, you know there isn't one revenue table. There are forty. Some are deprecated. Some are test data from 2022. Some are the right answer for finance and the wrong answer for marketing. A semantic layer says: when someone asks about revenue, use this one. Join it this way. Filter out internal accounts. And when they say "last quarter," resolve it to the fiscal calendar if they're in the sales org, calendar quarter if they're in product.

Without that map, your agent is guessing. With it, the LLM stops being a data scientist and becomes a translator, which is what it's actually good at.

## Put the context next to the field

Here's the part most teams get wrong. They write a big "company glossary" doc and stuff it into the system prompt. Then they wonder why the model still hallucinates.

Context works better when it lives next to the thing it describes. If you use Claude Code, you already know this: a CLAUDE.md at the repo root is fine, but a CLAUDE.md inside the directory it applies to is much better. Same principle for data. The definition of "qualified lead" should sit on the qualified_lead field itself, not three layers up in a README.

Practically, that means each field needs four things attached to it:

A human label. A description. An "AI context" note that explains how the field is typically used and what to combine it with. And sample values. That last one is sneakily important. If the region field contains AMER, EMEA, APAC, the model needs to see those exact strings. Otherwise a user types "United States" and the agent filters for "United States" against a column that only contains "AMER," and returns zero rows with total confidence.

## The fuzzy-match problem nobody plans for

Users typo. Users use synonyms. Users say "PRs" when the field is called pull_requests. Users say "the EU campaign" when the campaign name in HubSpot is EMEA_Q3_Brand_2025.

Your agent needs a step before SQL generation where it looks up actual values in the filter columns and does fuzzy matching against them. This is not optional. Skip it and your tool will return empty result sets for half the questions asked, and your CEO will conclude the AI is broken. They will be right.

## Why this matters for marketing operators specifically

If you're building any internal tool that lets non-analysts query marketing data, you are building a semantic layer whether you call it that or not. The question is whether you build it deliberately or accidentally.

Accidental version: prompt stuffed with rules, brittle, breaks every time someone renames a campaign. Deliberate version: definitions live with fields, sample values exposed to the model, ambiguous terms ("last quarter," "active customer," "paid traffic") resolved by user role or team context.

The teams shipping working internal analytics agents in 2026 aren't the ones with the best models. They're the ones who did the unsexy work of writing down what their words mean.

If I were starting this from scratch tomorrow at a mid-size company, I'd skip the chat UI for the first month entirely. I'd spend that time auditing the ten metrics the leadership team actually asks about in meetings, writing field-level definitions for each, and pulling sample values into a structured doc the agent can read. Then I'd wire up the chat. The catch most people miss is that this work doesn't end at launch. Definitions drift, campaigns get renamed, fiscal calendars shift. Whoever owns the semantic layer owns it forever, which means you should pick that person before you pick the LLM.
