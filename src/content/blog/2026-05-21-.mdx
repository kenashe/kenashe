Ask three people at your company what "last quarter" means and you'll get three answers. Product thinks calendar Q1. Sales thinks fiscal Q1 (which started in February for some reason). Finance has a 4-4-5 calendar that nobody outside finance can explain. They're all right inside their own context. They're all wrong everywhere else.

This is the problem nobody mentions when vendors pitch you "chat with your data." The model isn't the bottleneck. Claude, GPT, Gemini, they can all write SQL. The bottleneck is that your business definitions live in Slack threads, in someone's head, in a Notion doc from 2023, or worst of all, baked silently into a Looker view that nobody's audited in a year.

## The actual reason out-of-the-box LLMs hallucinate your analytics

Point an agent at a real warehouse and it sees 100 revenue tables. Forty-seven of them have a column called `amount`. Three of them are the right ones for ARR reporting, depending on whether you want booked, billed, or recognized. The model has no way to know this. It will pick something plausible. It will be wrong. The query will run. The number will look reasonable. You will present it in a board meeting.

This is the failure mode that kills internal AI reporting tools before they get past the demo. Demo databases have ten clean tables. Your warehouse has a hundred thousand columns and a graveyard of deprecated dbt models nobody deleted.

## What a semantic layer actually does

Strip away the jargon and a semantic layer is three things stitched together:

1. A curated map saying "these are the tables and fields that matter, ignore the rest."
2. Definitions for the business terms: what is a qualified lead, what counts as churn, when does a trial convert.
3. Context attached directly to those definitions, not stored somewhere else.

That third point is the one most teams get wrong. They write a beautiful glossary in Confluence and feed it to the model as a giant context blob. It sort of works. Then it stops working as definitions drift.

The pattern that holds up: localize context next to the field it describes. The same instinct that makes `CLAUDE.md` files work in coding agents works here. When the instruction lives next to the thing it governs, the model doesn't have to search for it. It doesn't have to choose between five conflicting definitions in a 40K-token system prompt.

## The four things to encode next to every field

If you're building this for your team, the minimum useful metadata per field is:

**Label and description.** What a human calls it. Boring but necessary.

**AI context.** Specifically written for a model. "When asked about pipeline, use this field, not `opportunity_amount`. Filter to stage greater than 2." Direct instructions, written like you'd write them for a junior analyst on day one.

**Sample queries.** "If someone asks for MQL-to-SQL conversion by source, here's the query shape." These do more work than any prose explanation. The model pattern-matches off them.

**Sample values.** This one is sneaky. If your `region` field contains `NAM`, `EMEA`, `APAC`, the model needs to see that. Otherwise a user types "United States" and the filter returns zero rows because the model guessed `US` or `USA`. A handful of example values fixes a huge class of fuzzy-matching failures.

## Where this breaks for marketing operators specifically

Marketing data is the worst-case scenario for this approach, and that's exactly why you need it most. UTMs are inconsistent. Campaign naming conventions changed three times. "Attributed revenue" means first-touch in one dashboard, multi-touch in another, and whatever HubSpot says in a third.

The temptation is to clean the data first, then add a semantic layer. Wrong order. Clean the data forever and you'll never ship. Instead, encode the mess into the semantic layer. "When asked about paid attribution, use `mta_revenue_v3`. Ignore `attributed_revenue` (legacy, do not use)." Make the layer carry the institutional memory so the warehouse doesn't have to.

The other thing that breaks: permissions. The moment an internal chat tool can answer "show me everyone's salary," you have a problem. Row-level and column-level permissions have to live in the semantic layer, not bolted on after. If you're building this from scratch, decide that on day one or you'll rip it out on day ninety.

## The feedback loop is the moat

Definitions change. Sales reorgs. Finance switches calendars. A new product line launches and "revenue" now means something slightly different. Any semantic layer that doesn't have a feedback loop, where the next question asked refines the definitions, decays into uselessness in about a quarter.

The teams getting real value out of internal data agents are the ones treating the semantic layer as a living artifact, not a one-time setup. Every wrong answer is a training signal. Every "actually, last quarter means fiscal here" gets encoded back into the field metadata.

If I were building an internal marketing reporting agent today, I'd start small: pick five questions the team actually asks every week. Hard-code the semantic definitions for the fields those questions touch. Ship it. Watch where it fails. Encode the fix next to the field. Repeat. The version that wins isn't the one with the most clever prompt, it's the one whose semantic layer reflects how your team actually talks about the business. Most builders will skip this part because it's tedious and unglamorous. That's the catch, and that's also the moat.
