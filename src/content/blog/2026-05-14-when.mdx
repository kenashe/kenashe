## The pitch is getting harder to ignore

The argument I keep hearing, and the one the source video pushes: SaaS pricing is built for a world where software was expensive to make. AI coding tools changed that. A tool that used to take a team of engineers six months can now be assembled by one person in a weekend, sometimes a day. So why are you paying $49/seat/month for a glorified form builder?

I find this convincing, but only halfway. Let me explain where I think it actually holds, and where the math quietly turns against you.

## Where building wins right now

For internal workflow tools, the case is strong. Things like: a dashboard that pulls campaign data from three sources, a small app that lets a VA tag inbound leads, a script that drafts client reports from Looker exports. These are jobs where the SaaS option is either overbuilt (you're paying for 80% of features you won't touch) or doesn't exist in the exact shape you need.

I've been building more of these myself with Claude Code and Cursor. A reporting tool that would have cost a $99/month subscription took me about four hours to scaffold. It does exactly what I need. Nothing more. No "upgrade to Pro for the feature you actually wanted."

The other clean win: tools that touch sensitive data. Client revenue numbers, ad account tokens, customer lists. Every SaaS you add is another vendor in your data path. Building locally means you own the surface.

## Where the build-it-yourself argument quietly falls apart

Three places, in my experience.

First, anything with a network effect or shared state. You're not going to rebuild Slack. You're not going to rebuild HubSpot's contact database that your sales team and your agency and your email platform all read from. The value isn't the code. It's that everyone else is already there.

Second, anything that requires ongoing trust infrastructure. Payment processing. Email deliverability. Auth at scale. You can stand up a Stripe integration in an hour, sure, but you can't recreate Stripe. The same is true for tools like Postmark or Sendgrid. The product is the relationships and reputation underneath, not the API.

Third, the maintenance tax. This is the one most "just build it" pitches skip over. The tool you built in a weekend needs updates when an API changes, when your team's needs shift, when something breaks at 11pm before a client report is due. SaaS prices in support. Your homemade version prices it into your evenings.

## The honest framework I'm using now

I've started running a simple test before paying for any new tool, or before building one:

If the SaaS costs under $30/month and does one job well, I just buy it. The build cost in attention is higher than the subscription. Examples: Cleanshot, Raycast Pro, a transcription tool.

If it costs $100 to $500/month and I'd use 30% of its features, I'm now seriously looking at building. This is the new zone AI opened up. A year ago this math didn't work. Today it sometimes does.

If it costs over $500/month and it's mission critical, I keep paying. The risk of a half-built internal tool failing during a launch is worth more than the savings. CRM, ad platforms, analytics, email infrastructure: not the place to play hero.

## What this actually changes for marketing operators

The shift I see coming, and I think this is the real story the video undersells: marketing teams will start to look more like small product teams. Not because everyone becomes a developer, but because the line between "ops person who uses tools" and "ops person who builds tools" is collapsing.

I'm watching agencies quietly assemble internal stacks of custom apps that nobody else has. A reporting tool tuned to one client's exact KPIs. A QA bot that checks ad copy against brand voice guidelines. A scraper that monitors competitor landing pages weekly. None of these are products. They're sharp edges on the work.

The competitive moat is shifting. It used to be which agency had the best paid SaaS stack. It's becoming which agency has the best internal stack that nobody can copy because nobody else built it.

## What I'm doing this week

Auditing every subscription under $200/month. Asking, for each one: would I rebuild this if I were starting today? If yes, keep it. If no, the question becomes when to cut it, not if.
