## The gap between how engineers test models and how marketers test prompts

Talk to anyone shipping serious AI products and you'll hear the same workflow. New model drops. They immediately run a battery of automated evals against it. A dashboard somewhere updates, showing the agent success rate climbing or dropping by some percentage. They know within hours whether the new model is better for their use case.

Now look at how most marketers test their AI workflows. They paste a prompt into Claude or ChatGPT, read the output, squint, mutter "yeah that's pretty good," and ship it. When a new model comes out, they run the same prompt, eyeball it, and decide based on feel. This is the vibe check, and it's how 90% of AI marketing automation gets evaluated right now.

The vibe check works fine when you're writing one blog post. It falls apart the second you build anything that runs at scale or in a loop. An agent that generates 200 outreach emails a week. A workflow that classifies inbound leads. A pipeline that summarizes call transcripts into CRM notes. You cannot vibe-check 200 emails. And without measurement, you have no idea if your last prompt change made things better or quietly worse.

## What an eval actually is (it's simpler than it sounds)

An eval is just three things: a set of test inputs, an expected behavior, and a way to score whether the output matched. That's it. The term sounds like ML jargon but the concept is the same one used by every QA team that ever existed.

For a marketing agent that classifies leads as "hot," "warm," or "cold," your eval looks like this. You collect 50 real lead descriptions. You hand-label each one with the right answer. You run them through your agent. You count how many it got right. If your prompt change moves you from 38/50 to 44/50, you have a real signal. If it moves you from 38/50 to 31/50, you caught a regression before your sales team did.

The scoring is the part people get stuck on. For classification tasks, it's trivial: did the label match. For generation tasks (writing emails, summaries, ad copy), you have two options. One, a rubric with manual scoring on a sample. Two, an LLM-as-judge setup where a second model grades the output against criteria you define. "Did this email mention the prospect's industry? Yes/no. Did it stay under 120 words? Yes/no. Did it include a specific CTA? Yes/no."

## A minimum viable eval stack for marketers

You don't need Braintrust or LangSmith to start. Here's what I'd build for a client tomorrow:

A Google Sheet with three tabs. Tab one is your test set: 30 to 50 input examples that represent the real distribution of cases your agent sees. Tab two is your expected outputs or rubric criteria. Tab three is your results log: model version, prompt version, score, date.

A simple script (Python or even a Make.com scenario) that pulls each input, runs your prompt, captures the output, and either compares to expected or sends it to a judge prompt for scoring. Append results to tab three.

Run it whenever you change the prompt. Run it whenever a new model ships. The whole thing takes a few hours to build the first time and 30 seconds to run after that.

The unlock is psychological as much as technical. Once you have a number, prompt iteration stops being a debate about taste and starts being a measured exercise. "I think this version is better" becomes "this version scores 86% vs the old 79%."

## Where evals quietly change your strategy

The interesting second-order effect is what evals reveal about your use cases. When you build a test set, you're forced to write down what success actually looks like. Most marketers have never done this for their AI workflows. They have a prompt and a vague sense of "good output." Building the eval forces specificity.

You also start noticing failure patterns. Maybe your lead classifier is great on SaaS leads and terrible on agency leads. Maybe your email generator nails B2B tone but defaults to weirdly formal phrasing for consumer brands. These patterns are invisible in vibe-check mode. They jump out the moment you have categorized test data.

And here's the part that matters for model selection: when Claude 4.5 or GPT-6 or whatever drops next month, you'll know in 10 minutes whether it's actually better for your specific workflow. Not "feels smarter on Twitter." Better, by your numbers, on your task.

The catch most people miss: your test set has to evolve. The first version will have blind spots. Every time your agent fails in production in a way the eval didn't catch, add that example to the test set. After six months of this, you have an asset that's worth more than the prompt itself. If I were building an AI marketing service business right now, I'd spend the first month not on prompts but on building eval infrastructure for each client's workflow. That's the moat, not the prompt library everyone is selling on Twitter.
